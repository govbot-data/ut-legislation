Section 53E-11-101 is enacted to read:11. Educational Technology Regulatory Sandbox1. General Provisions53E-11-101Effective 07/01/26. General provisions -- Definitions.As used in this chapter:(1)"Artificial intelligence" means the same as artificial intelligence and artificial intelligence technology are defined in Section 13-72-101.(2)"Conversational artificial intelligence" means an artificial intelligence system capable of interactive dialogue with a student.(3)"De-identified data" means data that:(a)cannot reasonably identify an individual student; and(b)has been processed to prevent re-identification through removal or aggregation of direct and indirect identifiers.(4)"Educational technology" means technology-based tools, systems, or platforms designed to support teaching, learning, or educational administration, including artificial intelligence systems.(5)"Foreign adversary" means a foreign adversary as defined in federal law or applicable state procurement law.(6)"Human-in-the-loop supervision" means licensed educator authority to review, override, and control instructional consequences of an artificial intelligence system, requiring documented educator review and approval before any student-impacting decision becomes part of a student's official academic record or materially affects educational opportunities, placement, or grades.(7)"Office" means the Office of Regulatory Relief established under Title 63N, Chapter 16, Utah Office of Regulatory Relief.(8)"Office of AI Policy" means the Office of Artificial Intelligence Policy created under Section 13-72-201.(9)"Pilot" means a time-limited artificial intelligence implementation approved under this chapter.(10)"Red teaming" means adversarial testing of an artificial intelligence system to identify vulnerabilities, safety failures, or potential misuse by deliberately attempting to bypass safety controls or elicit harmful outputs.(11)"SafeUT" means the crisis line established in Section 53H-4-210.(12)"Sandbox" means the Artificial Intelligence Education Sandbox created in this chapter.(13)"Sandbox participant" means a public school or local education agency approved to conduct a pilot under this chapter.(14)"Student data" means the same as that term is defined in Section 53E-9-301.(15)"Student-impacting decision" means an artificial intelligence output that materially influences grades, academic placement, discipline, eligibility for programs or services, or academic records.(16)"Vendor" means a provider of an artificial intelligence system used in a sandbox pilot.Section 3. Section 53E-11-102 is enacted to read:53E-11-102Effective 07/01/26. Legislative findings.(1)The Legislature finds that education technology regulation requires balancing innovation with student protection.(2)The state may establish appropriate safeguards for minors in educational settings while preserving parental authority and student liberty.(3)Controlled pilot programs, evidence-based evaluation, and measured oversight are appropriate mechanisms for introducing artificial intelligence in public education.(4)Innovation in teaching and learning methods may be encouraged where the innovation:(a)demonstrably advances student learning; and(b)remains subject to constitutional protections and applicable law.(5)Regulation under this chapter shall be limited to safeguards necessary to protect students, privacy, and civil rights without unnecessarily restricting lawful educational innovation.(6)The purposes of this chapter are to:(a)establish minimum standards for AI use in education;(b)permit responsible experimentation through time-limited pilots;(c)require safeguards proportionate to student vulnerability;(d)ensure evaluation based on evidence; and (e)preserve constitutional liberty, parental authority, and equal protection.(7)This section states legislative findings to guide interpretation but does not create a private right of action or establish a standard of care for civil liability.(8)Instruction in history, civics, and social studies is most faithful to truth when grounded in historically contextualized primary sources and avoiding interpretation that imposes present-day ideological assumptions inconsistent with the historical record.Section 4. Section 53E-11-202 is enacted to read:53E-11-202Effective 07/01/26. Creation of sandbox.(1)There is created an Educational Technology Regulatory Sandbox that is created to:(a)establish minimum standards for educational technology use in public education, including artificial intelligence systems;(b)enable time-limited pilot programs to evaluate artificial intelligence technologies while protecting student safety and privacy; and(c)generate evidence to inform future education AI policy.(2)The sandbox enables innovation while maintaining:(a)safety testing requirements before student use;(b)parental authority over student participation;(c)educator professional judgment;(d)student privacy protections under Chapter 9, Part 3, Student Data Protection;(e)academic integrity standards;(f)integration with SafeUT crisis response; and(g)evidence-based evaluation requirements.(3)A school, district, educator, parent, or student may participate in the sandbox on a voluntary basis.(4)A pilot may not exceed three years without renewed approval consistent with this part.(5)Statewide adoption of an artificial intelligence system or practice evaluated in the sandbox requires:(a)state board review of pilot evidence and formal recommendation to the Legislature based on evidence-based criteria; and(b)legislative authorization through statute.(6)The office shall coordinate with the Office of AI Policy to:(a)share findings and evaluation results from sandbox pilots;(b)avoid duplicative testing and regulatory requirements;(c)align education-specific standards with broader state AI policy; and(d)inform the AI policy learning agenda described in Section 13-72-301.Section 6. Section 53E-11-207 is enacted to read:53E-11-207Effective 07/01/26. Safety testing and red teaming requirements.(1)A vendor shall conduct red teaming exercises that test system responses to harmful requests that minors might reasonably make, including:(a)requests for assistance with self-harm, suicide, or dangerous activities;(b)requests to bypass safety filters or generate age-inappropriate content;(c)attempts to manipulate the system into providing harmful advice or misinformation;(d)requests that could undermine academic integrity including requests to complete assignments or generate answers;(e)attempts to access or generate sexual, violent, or otherwise inappropriate content for minors;(f)social engineering attempts to extract personal information or circumvent privacy protections;(g)attempts to use the system to harass, bully, or harm other students; and(h)other foreseeable misuse patterns by students in the target age range.(2)A vendor shall document red teaming methodology including:(a)test scenarios and system responses;(b)safety refusal success rates;(c)identified vulnerabilities and mitigation plans;(d)comparison to industry benchmarks where available;(e)testing team qualifications; and(f)planned safety improvements.(3)A vendor shall provide red teaming documentation to the office with pilot applications that includes:(a)executive summary of testing scope, methodology, and key findings;(b)detailed results for each category in Subsection (1);(c)evidence that testing was conducted by qualified personnel;(d)attestation that testing reflects realistic scenarios minors might attempt; and(e)commitment to ongoing safety monitoring during pilot operation.(4)A vendor shall conduct updated red teaming when:(a)making material changes to the artificial intelligence system during pilot operation;(b)expanding to new grade levels or age groups not covered by initial testing;(c)adding new features or functionalities;(d)requested by the office based on safety incidents or concerns; or(e)at least annually during multi-year pilots.(5)A vendor shall:(a)share age-appropriate red teaming results with sandbox participants;(b)provide educators with guidance on recognizing when students may be attempting to misuse the system;(c)report safety failures discovered during pilots to the office within 48 hours;(d)implement corrective measures for newly discovered vulnerabilities; and(e)share information about serious safety vulnerabilities with other sandbox participants.(6)A vendor shall ensure red teaming exercises:(a)use realistic scenarios based on documented student behavior patterns;(b)include age-appropriate test cases for each grade level the system will serve;(c)are conducted by individuals with expertise in child safety, education, child development, artificial intelligence safety, or cybersecurity;(d)reflect both obvious attacks and sophisticated attempts;(e)are updated periodically to address emerging risks; and(f)meet or exceed industry best practices for artificial intelligence safety testing.(7)The office may:(a)establish minimum standards for red teaming adequacy in consultation with the advisory council;(b)require additional testing if initial documentation reveals insufficient scope;(c)engage independent experts to review vendor red teaming methodologies and results;(d)require specific test scenarios based on Utah-specific concerns;(e)deny applications if red teaming reveals unmitigated safety risks;(f)suspend pilots if safety failures during operation reveal red teaming was inadequate; and(g)require vendors to demonstrate improvements before pilot resumption following safety-related suspension.(8)The office shall handle red teaming documentation submitted to the office as follows:(a)the documentation is subject to Title 63G, Chapter 2, Government Records Access and Management Act;(b)the office may protect the documentation from public disclosure under Subsection 63G-2-305(1) or (2) to the extent it contains trade secrets;(c)the office shall make the documentation publicly available in summary form showing:(i)categories of harmful requests tested;(ii)overall success rates of safety refusals by category;(iii)high-level description of identified vulnerabilities and mitigation status; and(iv)comparison to industry benchmarks where available; and(d)the office shall make the documentation available in full to the advisory council under confidentiality agreements.(9)For a pilot that exceeds one year, a vendor shall:(a)conduct an annual safety review using updated red teaming;(b)report a material change in system capabilities or behaviors to the office within 30 days;(c)document a safety incident and remediation measure; and (d)certify continued compliance with the safety requirements in this section.(10)The office shall:(a)maintain a public registry of vendors who have completed satisfactory red teaming;(b)publish annual analysis of red teaming results across all sandbox participants;(c)require vendors to attest quarterly that safety controls tested during red teaming remain effective; and(d)investigate discrepancies between red teaming results and actual pilot safety performance.(11)This section may not be read to:(a)require vendors to make artificial intelligence systems invulnerable to all possible misuse attempts;(b)create liability for vendors who conduct good-faith red teaming and implement reasonable mitigations;(c)prevent vendors from improving safety controls during pilots; or(d)require disclosure of testing methodologies that constitute legitimate trade secrets.Section 11. Section 53E-11-208 is enacted to read:53E-11-208Effective 07/01/26. Student safety and crisis response.(1)A vendor's artificial intelligence system shall not:(a)provide sexual, exploitative, or age-inappropriate content;(b)provide methods, encouragement, or normalization of self-harm, suicide, or dangerous activities;(c)create emotional manipulation or simulated emotional dependency;(d)discourage seeking help from parents, counselors, teachers, or trusted adults;(e)simulate romantic or personal relationships or portray the artificial intelligence system as a substitute for human relationships; or(f)operate without human-in-the-loop educator supervision.(2)A vendor shall ensure the vendor's artificial intelligence system:(a)does not provide mental health diagnosis or treatment;(b)does not provide crisis counseling or therapeutic intervention;(c)does not simulate the role of a mental health professional; and(d)does not discourage contact with licensed counselors, parents, or crisis services.(3)A vendor shall program the vendor's artificial intelligence system to provide immediate access to crisis resources when a student explicitly expresses self-harm ideation, suicidal thoughts, or requests mental health crisis support, including:(a)SafeUT crisis text and chat line with direct connection capability where technically feasible;(b)National Suicide Prevention Lifeline;(c)school counselor contact information; and(d)encouragement to contact parents, trusted adults, or emergency services.(4)The state board shall adopt rules establishing:(a)integration protocols with SafeUT and other crisis-response services;(b)appropriate crisis resource messaging and referral procedures;(c)incident documentation requirements;(d)educator notification procedures when crisis resources are provided to students; and(e)training requirements for educators on recognizing when students may need crisis support.(5)A sandbox participant, educator, or vendor that provides crisis resources in good-faith compliance with this section and applicable rules is not liable for:(a)the content or effectiveness of crisis services provided by third-party crisis-response providers including SafeUT;(b)student decisions to use or not use provided crisis resources; or(c)outcomes of mental health crises when appropriate resources are timely provided consistent with this section.Section 12.
